# Kuwaiti Public Opinion on the Iran 2026 War

## Overview

This repository presents a data-driven analysis of public opinion in Kuwait regarding the Iran 2026 War, based on Arabic-language social media data.

The project applies a structured OSINT-based analytical framework to examine how political narratives, threat perceptions, and regional dynamics are constructed and circulated in digital public discourse.

---

## Research Focus

This study investigates:

- How Kuwaiti public opinion perceives the Iran 2026 War
- The dominant political narratives shaping discussion
- Attitudes toward Iran, the United States, Israel, and regional security
- Public reactions to US military bases in Kuwait
- The role of the Persian Gulf (خلیج فارس) and energy security in shaping discourse

---

## Data Description

- Source: Social media platform X (Twitter)
- Language: Arabic
- Timeframe: Late February 2026 – Early April 2026
- Number of records: ~29,000 rows
- Unique posts: ~1,800
- Unique users: ~1,600

### Important Note

The dataset contains significant duplication due to platform structure (retweets, reposts, scraping overlaps). No rows are removed; duplication is explicitly analyzed as part of the information environment.

---

## Methodology

The analysis follows a multi-layer pipeline:

1. Data audit and structure analysis
2. Metadata and engagement analysis
3. Topic classification (mutually exclusive)
4. Narrative co-occurrence mapping
5. Stance analysis (positive vs negative, no neutral category)
6. Temporal trend analysis
7. Actor-type comparison (official vs public)
8. Strategic interpretation

---

## Key Findings (Summary)

- Iran is the central narrative node and is overwhelmingly perceived as a direct threat
- Public discourse is strongly security-driven rather than ideological
- The United States is perceived ambivalently: both as a security provider and a source of risk
- Israel is viewed negatively but is not the primary narrative focus
- The Persian Gulf (خلیج فارس) and energy routes are critical in shaping risk perception
- Public opposition to continued conflict is dominant
- Narratives are driven more by influencers and public users than official accounts
