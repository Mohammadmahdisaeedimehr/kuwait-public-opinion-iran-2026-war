# Actor-Type Comparison Summary

The actor-type comparison shows that the discourse is not primarily driven by official or institutional accounts.

Analysts and influencers are the dominant narrative-shaping group. They produce 46.63 percent of total content and account for 59.03 percent of total impressions, indicating that they play the strongest role in framing the crisis and amplifying political interpretations.

Ordinary users represent the second-largest group by content volume, producing 37.96 percent of total content. Their higher reply rate suggests that this group captures grassroots anxiety, debate, and social reaction more strongly than formal narrative control.

Media and news accounts play a stabilizing and distributive role. They help circulate and normalize crisis-related information, but they do not dominate visibility compared with analysts and influencers.

Official and institutional accounts produce very little content, but their average views per post are high. This suggests that institutional actors retain symbolic visibility, even if they do not shape the main volume of discourse.

Overall, the Kuwaiti discourse is shaped by a combination of influencer-led interpretation and ordinary-user reaction rather than centralized official messaging.
